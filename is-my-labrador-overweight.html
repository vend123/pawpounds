Is my Labrador overweight?

Is my Labrador overweight?

If your Lab looks a little generous lately, you're not imagining it. Labradors are consistently near the top of every overweight-breed ranking vets publish — and there's a genuine genetic reason behind those pleading eyes at dinner.

Estimates put 40–60% of pet Labradors in the overweight or obese range. The good news: a Lab's ideal weight is well defined, and the fixes are simple once you know the target.

Labrador ideal weight

Labs are bigger than they look under the muscle and coat. Use this as a healthy-adult target, then confirm with the hands-on checks — frame size varies, so shape matters more than the exact number.

Healthy adult Labrador weight (12+ months)
SexIdeal rangeRough average
Male29–36 kg (65–80 lb)~32 kg
Female25–32 kg (55–70 lb)~28 kg

Working-line Labs sit at the lean end; show-line (English) Labs are stockier and may sit higher without being fat.

The Labrador gene, for real. Around a quarter of Labradors carry a deletion in a gene called POMC that blunts the “I'm full” signal and ramps up food motivation. It's why Labs are famously food-obsessed — and why the same treat does more damage to them than to most breeds. It's not greed; it's biology. Which means portion discipline matters more for a Lab than almost any other dog.

How to tell if your Lab is overweight

  • Feel for ribs. Lay both hands flat on the ribcage. You should feel ribs easily under a thin layer. Having to press means excess fat.
  • Look from above. You want a visible waist tucking in behind the ribs. A straight or bulging outline means no waist.
  • Check the side profile. The belly should tuck up behind the ribcage. A level or sagging tummy is a red flag.

What to do about it

  • Weigh the food, don't scoop it. “One cup” by eye is usually 20–30% too much.
  • Count treats as calories. Keep them under 10% of daily intake; swap biscuits for carrot or green bean.
  • Ignore the eyes. That stare is the POMC gene talking, not real hunger.
  • Reweigh monthly. Safe loss is about 1–2% of body weight per week.

Frequently asked

How much should a full-grown Labrador weigh?

Most healthy adult males land between 29–36 kg (65–80 lb) and females between 25–32 kg (55–70 lb). Frame size varies, so use the body-shape checks to confirm rather than chasing an exact number.

Why is my Labrador always hungry?

Around a quarter of Labradors carry a POMC gene deletion that dampens the feeling of fullness and increases food drive. For these dogs the constant hunger is genuinely wired in, which is exactly why measured portions matter so much.

Is my Lab fat or just big-boned?

Big frame and fat feel different under the hands. If you can feel ribs easily and see a waist from above, a heavier number is likely just size. If ribs are buried and the waist is gone, it's weight.

How fast can a Labrador safely lose weight?

Around 1–2% of body weight per week. Faster risks muscle loss; go slow, recheck monthly, and involve your vet for an overweight dog.
